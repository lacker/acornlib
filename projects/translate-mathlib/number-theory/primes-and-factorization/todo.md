# Prime Numbers and Unique Factorization

Goal: round out the prime-number API on `Nat` and `Int` and add prime factorisation (as a list of primes) with the fundamental theorem of arithmetic.

- [ ] Point-wise GCD characterisation: `count_prime_factor(p, gcd(a, b)) = min(count_prime_factor(p, a), count_prime_factor(p, b))` for nonzero `a, b`
- [ ] Point-wise LCM characterisation: `count_prime_factor(p, lcm(a, b)) = max(count_prime_factor(p, a), count_prime_factor(p, b))` for nonzero `a, b`

Notes:

- `src/nat/nat_base.ac` already has `is_prime`, `is_composite`, `has_prime_divisor`, the infinitude theorem `exists_infinite_primes`, and Euclid's lemma form `gcd_of_prime`. `Nat.coprime` and `coprime_divides_of_divides_mul` (the modern Euclid's lemma) live in `src/nat/nat_coprime.ac`.
- `src/int/lattice.ac` defines `is_prime(a)` (top-level, not attribute) `:= abs(a).is_prime` and proves `gcd_of_prime` and `euclids_lemma_prime` for `Int`. The Nat-Int prime bridges `nat_is_prime_iff_int_is_prime` and `int_is_prime_iff_abs_is_prime` are in `src/int/int_divisibility.ac`.
- For the fundamental theorem: prefer the **list-of-primes** representation as the primary one (matches `nat_crt_list` / `list_product` shape and works with the existing `pairwise_coprime` and `product[Nat]` infrastructure); derive `count_prime_factor` from it. The multiset-via-`v_p(n)` view is the natural alternative form once divisibility lattices are needed.
- For genericity: keep prime factorisation in a new file `src/nat/nat_factorisation.ac` so the heavy structural-induction lemmas don't inflate `nat_base.ac`'s cache footprint. The downstream `Int` prime API can layer on top via the absolute-value bridge.
- `src/nat/nat_factorisation.ac` is now in place with `prime_factor_dichotomy`, `prime_divisor_is_one_or_self`, `coprime_of_distinct_primes`, the `all_prime` predicate with structural lemmas (`all_prime_nil`, `all_prime_cons_intro`, `all_prime_cons_elim`), `prime_factorisation_exists` (every positive Nat is the product of some list of primes, by strong induction), the named function `prime_factorisation(n)` (a satisfy-witness with the empty-list placeholder for n = 0), the user-facing `prime_factorisation_product`, `prime_factorisation_all_prime`, `prime_factorisation_zero` accessors, plus the no-shared-prime-factor characterisation: `coprime_imp_no_shared_prime_factor` (forward direction), `not_coprime_imp_shared_prime_factor` (backward direction with cases for both-zero and gcd > 1), and the user-facing biconditional `coprime_iff_no_shared_prime_factor`. The trial-division primality test `prime_iff_no_proper_divisor` plus its directional split `prime_imp_no_proper_divisor`/`no_proper_divisor_imp_prime` and the universal-instance helper `prime_imp_no_proper_divisor_at` give a sieve-friendly characterisation of primality. The strong-induction body needed `let h: Bool = q < k implies f(q); h` to make the prover instantiate the `true_below` hypothesis at the recursive call site.
- The uniqueness side of the fundamental theorem now lives as `prime_factorisation_unique` in `src/nat/nat_factorisation.ac`: any two `all_prime` lists with equal product are permutations (in the `is_permutation` sense). Supporting lemmas: `all_prime_remove_one_cons_eq`, `all_prime_remove_one_cons_neq`, `all_prime_remove_one`, `prime_does_not_divide_one`, `all_prime_product_eq_one_imp_nil`, `prime_divides_product_cons_step`, `prime_divides_product_imp_contains` (Euclid's lemma extended to lists of primes), `all_prime_append`, `all_prime_only_primes`.
- Generic list-permutation infrastructure lives in `src/list/list_permutation.ac`: `List[T].remove_one(item)` removes the first occurrence; `is_permutation[T](a, b) := forall(x). a.count(x) = b.count(x)` is the count-based permutation predicate; `permutation_refl/symm/trans`; `remove_one_cons_eq/neq`, `remove_one_count_self/other`, `remove_one_not_contains`; `nil_count_zero`; `list_count_all_zero_imp_nil`; `permutation_nil_imp_nil`; `permutation_remove_one`; `product_remove_one[A: CommMonoid]` and the headline `permutation_preserves_product[A: CommMonoid]`; the append helpers `count_append[T]` and `product_append[A: CommMonoid]`. Proofs in this file generalise to any element type, but cross-file inductive proofs in `nat_factorisation.ac` needed care: hot proofs explode the search space, so structurally each big induction was split into a small cons-step lemma (`all_prime_remove_one_cons_eq/neq`, `prime_divides_product_cons_step`) plus a thin induction body that calls `List.induction(...)` then `forall(l) { p(l) }; p(list)` explicitly. Substitution under `if x = head` requires writing `tail.count(x) = tail.count(head)` as an explicit step rather than relying on the prover to discover it.
- `count_prime_factor(p, n) := prime_factorisation(n).count(p)` lives in `src/nat/nat_factorisation.ac`. Bridge `count_prime_factor_invariant` derives the count from any all-prime witness via `prime_factorisation_unique`. Identities: `count_prime_factor_one`, `count_prime_factor_self`, `count_prime_factor_non_prime`, `count_prime_factor_other_prime` (count of one prime in a different prime is zero). Multiplicativity over nonzero arguments: `count_prime_factor_mul`. The multiplicativity proof is the canonical pattern: combine the two factorisations with `+`, apply `all_prime_append` and `product_append` to make it a valid factorisation of `a*b`, then read off counts via `count_append` and the invariant lemma.
- The alternative form of the fundamental theorem and the divisibility characterisation are in `src/nat/nat_factorisation.ac`. `count_prime_factor_ext`: nonzero `a, b` are equal iff they have identical prime counts on every prime (non-primes handled via `count_prime_factor_non_prime`). `divides_imp_count_prime_factor_le` (forward) and `count_prime_factor_le_imp_divides` (backward) combine into `divides_iff_count_prime_factor_le`. The backward direction induction lives in `product_all_prime_divides` (and its cons step `product_all_prime_divides_step`), bootstrapped by `count_divisor_quotient` to produce a quotient `q` from `head` divides `b` via `product_remove_one`. The pattern of `if cond { conclusion }` immediately followed by re-asserting the conclusion is needed to instantiate a previously derived universally quantified implication at a specific value.
